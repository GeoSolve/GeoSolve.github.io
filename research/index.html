Glacier and mélange dynamics

Ice discharge at or close to the glacier front is a major process contributing to recent ice loss in Greenland. Marine-terminating glaciers are generally more variable than other glaciers. I combine satellite imagery and terrestrial radar interferometry (TRI) to map and analyze speed and elevation changes of the glacier terminus. See details in Xie et al. (2016, 2018).

The video on the right (credit: Denise Holland, New York University) shows a calving event at Jakobshavn Isbræ that produces icebergs. Using high temporal resolution TRI data, we detected precursor motion several days before the actual calving event.

Fault slip rate

Accurate estimation of fault slip rate is fundamental to seismic hazard assessment. Surface displacement measured by GPS or Interferometric synthetic aperture radar (InSAR) can be used to invert for fault slip rate on decadal time scales (geodetic slip rate). Measured surface displacement and dated offset landforms can define slip rate over longer time scales (geologic slip rate). These two methods do not always agree. We investigated limitations in both these methods and found that the geodetic versus geologic “discrepancy” in the Eastern California Shear Zone (ECSZ) is an artifact of limited data and underestimated uncertainty in geologic fault slip rates (Xie et al. 2018).
